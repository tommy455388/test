Main PeakLocated in the center of Taiwan, 3,952 meters above sea level, it is the highest mountain in Taiwan, and the highest peak out of the 100 Peaks of Taiwan. It is also the highest peak in Northeast Asia.

East PeakLocated on the east side of the main peak of Yushan, 3,869 meters above sea level, it is one of the peaks in the Yushan, and the third highest peak out of the 100 Peaks of Taiwan.

North PeakThe North Peak of Yushan is 3858 meters high, and was called North Mountain, New High North Mountain, and Douliu New High Peak during the Japanese colonial era. period. It is one peak away from Yushan’s Northern North Peak, and is shaped like a camel’s hump, and was called Tiantuo Peak (“camel peak”) by Taiwanese mountaineer Xing Tian-Zheng.

South PeakThe North Peak of Yushan is 3,844 meters high and was known as South Mountain and New High South Mountain during the Japanese rule. It is also known as Tianlong Peak (“dragon peak”) because of its resemblance to a flying dragon when viewed from the Main Peak.

Located in the central part of Taiwan, Yushan National Park has a subtropical climate with temperate alpine and cold climate zone. It snows in winter and is cool in summer. The elevation ranges from 300 meters in the eastern Lakulaku River valley to 3,952 meters of the Main Peak, and the temperature varies greatly with the altitude. The four seasons of Yushan are distinct and beautiful: the azaleas bloom in spring, there are cloud seas in summer, oxeye daisies bloom in autumn, and the snow is as white as jade in winter, making “Yushan Snow” one of the eight scenic attractions in Taiwan.

The topography and climate of Yushan National Park are richly varied, giving rise to a variety of animal and plant species, and a wealth of ecological resources.

Alpine Vegetation ZoneMainly located above 3,600 meters, common plants include azalea, flaky juniper, and Morrison stonecrop.

Fir ForestIt is located between 3,100 and 3,600 meters above sea level, i.e. below the alpine vegetation zone and near the top of the mountain ridge, where the sun’s radiation is extremely strong. Common plants include Taiwan fir.

Formosan Black BearTaiwan’s endemic Asian black bear subspecies. The V-shaped white markings on the chest are a common feature of Asian black bears.

Formosan Sambar DeerThe Formosan sambar deer is a subspecies endemic to Taiwan. It is a crepuscular animal, and inhabits primeval forests at mid to high altitudes above 1,000 meters in elevation.

Yellow-throated MartenIt has a bright yellow neck, black feet and tail, white jaws and throat, and a long, cylindrical body. It inhabits broad-leaved forests, coniferous forests, and umbrella bamboo forests.

Descriptions found in historical texts include a statement in a book titled “Supplementary Notes on the Wild Territories” written by Yu Yonghe during the reign of Emperor Kangxi of the Qing Dynasty (1697): “Yushan is king among ten thousand mountains. Its mountains are unique and high. It can be seen from afar. It is steep and craggy. It is white as silver. From afar, it looks like snow from the heavens. It is surrounded by peaks on all sides. It is unreachable. It is said that this mountain is as solid as jade.”
During the Japanese colonial era in Taiwan, the Japanese found that Yushan was taller than Mount Fuji in Japan, so in the 30th year of the Meiji era, the Emperor Meiji named it “Mount Niitaka”. During the Republic of China era, the name of the mountain was announced as Yushan on December 1, 1947.
Yushan has been a very important mountain for the indigenous people since ancient times, and is considered a sacred mountain by the Bunun and Tsou people.

BununThe Bunun people call Yushan “Tongu Saviec”, meaning “mountain of refuge”, because the Bunun say that in ancient times, when the land was flooded, only Yushan still offered shelter.

TsouAccording to legend, their ancestors lived at the top of Yushan and called it “pattonkan”, which transliterated to “Batongguan” in Chinese. It means “shining mountain” or “mountain of quartz”.

Nearby AttractionsSpanning the four counties of Nantou, Chiayi, Hualien and Kaohsiung, the Yushan National Park is the largest national park in Taiwan, covering a vast area and surrounded by a diversity of geographical landscapes.

  • Jiaming Lake
    Located at an altitude of 3,300 meters, Jiaming Lake is called “the mirror of the moon” by the Bunun people; because of the deep blue color of the lake. It is called “God's lost sapphire on earth” and “angel's tears” by the mountaineering community. It has become a famous mountain attraction in Taiwan in recent years.
  • Tataka
    In the language of the Tsou people, it means a wide, flat expanse of grassland. Tataka is the highest point on the New Central Cross-Island Highway at an altitude of 2,610 meters, and is located in the northwest part of Yushan National Park.
    The Tataka area has a view of the Main Peak and surrounding high peaks of Yushan Main to the east, and the Zhushan and Tashan mountain ranges to the west across the Shenmu River. In the early mornings and evenings, cloud seas and mountain mists often form in the mountain valleys.
  • Walami Trail
    Walami Trail is located in the eastern part of the Yushan National Park, and it is 4-kilometer in length. The trail was planned along the Batongguan Trail built by the Japanese, and forms a part of the eastern section of the Batongguan Trail.
  • Batongguan
    Also known as “Pantounkua” or “Pattonkuan” in the olden days, it refers to the peaks and mountains in the Yushan area. The Batongguan Trail was originally the “Batongguan Mountain Pass” during the Japanese rule, and was built in 1921 as a trail for the Japanese to implement the Native Management Policy and to connect the eastern and western parts of Taiwan. It is currently listed as a National Historic Monument.

Source: Wikipedia, Yushan National Park, National Parks of Taiwan
